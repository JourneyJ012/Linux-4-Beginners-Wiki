## Linux Mint
You will be greeted by a login screen. Enter your password and launch. Feel free to go through each of the apps in the first step. Take as much time as you want customizing your desktop colors.

It is recommended to set up System Snapshots. The best place to put these are in a 2nd disk connected to your computer. `rsync` is a full copy, whilst `btrfs` is only on the changes. If `btrfs` isn't choosable, go for `rsync`. I recommend keeping at least 1 weekly backup and 1 daily backup, but it really depends on how much you value your machine. Too many backups can become an issue, and zero backups mean that it's easier for your machine to become somewhat unfixable if you screw something up.

Next, launch the driver manager. If you have no drivers needed, you're all good. Otherwise, install the one marked "Recommended".

Next up is the Update Manager. You may have a large amount of updates pending. A unique function of Linux is that the updates present here include both your software and your operating system at once, meaning you don't have to update every app one at a time. Choose to install the updates and minimise the updater. It is recommended to update everything at least once a week. 

Next up is the system settings. This is where you can change almost everything about your system. Feel free to customise further in the "Appearance" options, add your Online accounts, choose your preferred applications, and add some extra panels. If anything seems immediately wrong, such as your screen looking wrong or your mouse being too quick, you can fix these here.

To install apps, check out the software manager. Feel free to install some applications to use later. For gamers, Steam and Heroic Games Launcher is present. For Home Offices, both LibreOffice and OnlyOffice are here. VLC is a fan favorite, alongside Blender for 3D modellers and Spotify too.

The firewall here is fairly simple. Set it to "Office" or "Home" for a generally unobtrusive experience.
